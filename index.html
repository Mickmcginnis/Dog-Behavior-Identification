Dog Behavior Identification

Value Creation

According to Lendedu.com, one in three Fortune 500 companies offer pet healthcare insurance to employees as a voluntary benefit [1], indicating that pet health and wellness is of significant concern in modern society. This reality has given rise to a number of premium pet technology such as PetChatz [2], an interactive two-way pet camera, and Fitbark [3], a wearable canine health tracker.

PetChatz utilizes video calling technology to enable users to check up on and remotely interact with their pets. The camera module features a video display that enables pets to view their owners through the PetChatz app, functioning much like a dedicated Zoom- or Facetime-enabled smart device. The system also features a treat dispenser as a reward mechanism.

According to the PetChatz.com Benefits page, the PetChatz interactive webcam and reward system enables users to maintain connection with their pets throughout the day. Owners can remotely check in on their pets to encourage socialization, monitor them, and engage with them to combat pet boredom and loneliness. PetChatz claims to offer users "peace of mind" by enabling them to check up on their pets at any time to ensure their "safety and security" [4].

Similarly, Fitbark also enables users to monitor their pets (specifically dogs) remotely to ensure their health and safety. The Fitbark is a small, lightweight device with motion-sensing capabilities that attaches to a collar or harness and connects to the user's device via mobile app. The mobile app syncs with the Fitbark wearable and supplies users with exercise and sleep data collected from their dog's movement throughout the day. The data is then presented in the health index feature, which alerts users to exercise or sleep abnormalities that may be indicative of canine illness [5].

Like Petchatz, Fitbark enables users to monitor their dog's health and activity remotely. However, instead of using video capabilities, Fitbark uses motion data to continuously monitor trends in movement and advise users on pet health and wellness. Data collected from the collar attachment is compared to data previously collected from the same dog to identify shifts in activity that may be indicative of new health issues as well as data collected from other dogs of the same breed, which can be used to identify abnormalities indicative of ongoing health problems [6].

Similar to PetChatz and Fitbark, our product seeks to tap into the growing market for premium pet technology. It will create value by offering users insight into their pet's behavior through remote monitoring using motion data, specifically by identifying movements of interest, such as lying down and jumping up. By recording and analyzing this data, the product will present users not only with an overview of their pet's activity and movement useful for estimating their pet's overall health, but also with specific behaviors their pet may be exhibiting for training purposes. Dynamics

Data was collected using smart phone accelerometer readings recorded via the Matlab mobile app. For the duration of data collection, the phone was kept in place on the dog's back by a pocket on the back of a sweater typically worn by the dog for warmth. The phone was positioned flat on the dog's back with the screen facing upward, its y-axis parallel to the dog's spine, its x-axis perpendicular to the dog's spine, and its z-axis vertical and perpendicular to the platform of the dog's back (see Figure 1).

Next steps

The next steps in developing the product would involve repeating the process detailed above on data collected from other dogs, preferably of different sizes, breeds, and temperaments. Similar filtering and frequency analysis would be performed to isolate relevant data from noise; the refined data would then be used to further train the algorithm and improve accuracy in behavior identification. The number of behaviors identified would also ideally be increased to include running and walking. This would offer the user further insight into their dog's common behavior, energy level, health, and training progress.

Several design changes could be made to improve the long-term comfort of the device; namely, a more breathable vest or harness could be used for dogs in warmer climates or with longer fur, or perhaps a dedicated motion sensor could be used so that the size of the device could be reduced and possible attached to a collar for added comfort. Better still, a special collar with an embedded motion sensor could be created, resulting in a more secure fit and therefore more accurate data, as the sensor would be less inclined to bounce if the mass were more evenly distributed. This might also increase comfort, as the dog would not feel the weight of the sensor as much were the mass not concentrated so densely.

Additional exploration of the usage of this device might involve interactions with other specialized components to accomplish a more specific goal. For example, the device might be used along with an automated reward system so that when the sensor identifies desirable behavior under certain conditions, the dog may be rewarded (for a specific use case, refer to Example 2).
